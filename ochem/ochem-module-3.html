01 · The Synthesis

You Have the Alphabet. You Have the Words. Now Read.

You know how to count carbons from a skeleton. You know what an —OH group means, what an amine does, what an aromatic ring tells you about lipophilicity. Those were the pieces.

Module 3 is where the pieces become a language. A complete molecule is a sentence — a carbon skeleton with functional groups attached, each one contributing a clause to the story of how that drug behaves.

Reading a molecule is reading a prediction. Before you ever open a pharmacology textbook, the structure is already telling you: where I go, how fast I work, how long I last, how the body will remove me. — Molecular Literacy, Module 3

In this module we will read four real drugs — completely, step by step — and then you will try one on your own. By the end, you will be able to look at a structure and tell a story about it.

Reading Protocol

Every time you read a new molecule, follow this sequence:

1. Find the skeleton. Count the carbons. Note the chain length or ring structures.

2. Find the functional groups. Scan for O, N, double bonds, rings. Name each one.

3. Read the behavior. Each group tells you something. Put it together.

02 · Step-by-Step Reads

Four Drugs, Read Completely

Each molecule is decoded using the three-step reading protocol. Work through all four tabs.

Propofol
General anesthetic · GABA-A agonist · Your induction agent
⬡ aromatic ring —OH alcohol carbon skeleton
⬡—OH
(benzene ring)
with two isopropyl arms:
—CH(CH₃)₂  on each side
  • Step 1 · Skeleton The core is a benzene ring — six carbons in a hexagon. On each side, there's an isopropyl group: a carbon with two methyl groups branching off it. That's 6 + 3 + 3 = 12 carbons total. Short chain, compact molecule.
  • Step 2 · Groups Aromatic ring: makes propofol lipophilic — fat-loving. This is what allows it to cross the blood-brain barrier rapidly. Hydroxyl —OH: makes it just water-soluble enough to be formulated as an injectable. Without the OH, it couldn't be administered IV.
  • Step 3 · Behavior Lipophilic ring = fast brain entry. —OH = injectable despite fat-solubility. The two bulky isopropyl arms create steric hindrance — they physically block the metabolism enzymes from reaching the molecule too quickly, extending its effect. Onset: 30–60 seconds. That's the sentence.
Clinical Sentence
"Propofol is a compact lipophilic molecule that crosses the blood-brain barrier in seconds thanks to its aromatic ring, stays injectable thanks to its —OH group, and has its duration controlled by the bulky arms that slow metabolism."
Lidocaine
Local anesthetic · Sodium channel blocker · Also antiarrhythmic
⬡ aromatic ring —N— amine C=O carbonyl
⬡ —C(=O)— NH— CH₂— N(C₂H₅)₂
(aromatic · amide · diethylamine)
  • Step 1 · Skeleton Lidocaine has a benzene ring at one end and a small carbon chain in the middle, ending in a nitrogen with two ethyl groups attached. It's an "amide-type" local anesthetic — meaning the link between the ring and the chain is an amide bond (C=O next to NH).
  • Step 2 · Groups Aromatic ring: lipophilicity, membrane penetration. Amide (C=O + NH): the linkage that makes lidocaine resistant to plasma esterases — unlike ester anesthetics, this bond doesn't get cleaved in the blood. Tertiary amine (N with two ethyl arms): the pH-sensitive mechanism — uncharged at tissue pH, crosses membrane; becomes charged inside cell, blocks sodium channel.
  • Step 3 · Behavior The amide bond = longer duration than ester anesthetics (not cleaved by plasma enzymes). The amine = the actual blocking mechanism. The ring = penetrates nerve membranes. This is why lidocaine lasts 1–2 hours and works so reliably. The whole molecule is engineered by its groups.
Clinical Sentence
"Lidocaine's amide bond resists plasma enzymes for durability, its aromatic ring enables membrane penetration, and its amine switches between charged and uncharged states to cross the nerve membrane and block sodium channels from inside."
Succinylcholine
Depolarizing neuromuscular blocker · RSI drug · Fastest onset/offset
—COO— ester (x2) —N⁺— quaternary amine (x2) short chain
(CH₃)₃N⁺ —CH₂CH₂— OOC —CH₂CH₂— COO— CH₂CH₂— N⁺(CH₃)₃
(two choline units joined by a succinate bridge)
  • Step 1 · Skeleton Succinylcholine is essentially two acetylcholine molecules joined end-to-end by a short carbon bridge. Short chain throughout — no rings. This water-soluble, compact structure means it gets distributed quickly and eliminated quickly.
  • Step 2 · Groups Two ester bonds (—COO—): plasma pseudocholinesterases target these immediately. The moment succinylcholine enters the bloodstream, enzymes begin hydrolysing those ester bonds. Two quaternary amines (N⁺): permanently charged — can't cross the blood-brain barrier or cell membranes. Stays in the neuromuscular junction.
  • Step 3 · Behavior Permanent charge = can only work at the NMJ, nowhere else. Two ester bonds = enzymatic destruction begins immediately. This is why you get 8–12 minutes of paralysis and then it's gone. You cannot reverse it — you wait for the esters to be cleaved. The molecule is its own timer.
Clinical Sentence
"Succinylcholine's two ester bonds are its countdown clock — plasma enzymes start cleaving them the moment it enters blood — and its permanent positive charge keeps it locked at the neuromuscular junction, giving you fast paralysis with a predictable, brief duration."
Sevoflurane
Volatile inhalation anesthetic · Smooth induction · Fast wake-up
—F fluorine atoms (x7) ether linkage tiny skeleton
F₃C —CH(F)—O—CH₂F
(fluoromethyl 2,2,2-trifluoro-1-(trifluoromethyl)ethyl ether)
7 fluorine atoms · 4 carbons · 1 oxygen
  • Step 1 · Skeleton Tiny molecule — only 4 carbons and 1 oxygen. An ether linkage (oxygen bridging two carbon chains) forms the backbone. What's unusual is how many fluorine atoms are attached: 7 of them, replacing nearly all the hydrogens. This is the story of sevoflurane.
  • Step 2 · Groups Fluorine atoms: fluorine is the most electronegative element. Replacing hydrogens with fluorine makes the molecule highly stable (hard to break down), very volatile (becomes a gas easily at room temperature), and poorly soluble in blood. Low blood solubility is the key to everything here. Ether oxygen: contributes to anesthetic potency — ethers have been used as anesthetics since the 1800s.
  • Step 3 · Behavior Low blood solubility = the drug doesn't dissolve into blood — it goes straight to the brain and straight back out. This is why sevoflurane has a fast on and fast off. Patients wake up quickly, smoothly, with minimal hangover. The seven fluorine atoms are the reason. The molecule is almost entirely made of them.
Clinical Sentence
"Sevoflurane's seven fluorine atoms make it so poorly soluble in blood that it behaves almost like a pure gas — rushing to the brain on induction and leaving just as fast on emergence, producing one of the cleanest wake-ups in modern anesthesia."

Ketamine — Read the Structure
Ketamine is a dissociative anesthetic. You have never studied its structure formally. Use your reading skills. Select all correct statements.
Ketamine (simplified):
— Aromatic ring (benzene) with a Cl (chlorine) attached
— C=O carbonyl group (ketone)
— N—CH₃ secondary amine
— Short carbon chain connecting ring to amine
— No ester bonds · No —OH group
— 13 carbons total
The aromatic ring suggests ketamine is lipophilic and capable of crossing the blood-brain barrier.
The ester bond means ketamine will be metabolized quickly by plasma cholinesterases.
The amine group (N—CH₃) will be pH-sensitive, changing charge state across membranes.
The absence of an —OH group means ketamine cannot be administered intravenously.
The C=O carbonyl is a reactive site — likely where liver metabolism begins.
Ketamine's 13-carbon chain makes it highly water-soluble like short-chain molecules.
