01 · The Concept

Words Give the Skeleton Meaning

In Module 1 you learned the alphabet — how to read the carbon skeleton of any molecule. You can count carbons, find hidden hydrogens, and decode a line structure from scratch.

But a skeleton by itself doesn't tell you much. A chain of carbons is just a chain of carbons. It becomes interesting — and chemically alive — when something gets attached to it.

Those attachments are called functional groups. They are the words of molecular language. Each one has a specific shape you learn to recognize on sight, and each one carries a predictable meaning: how this molecule will dissolve, react, bind, and behave.

The Key Insight

Two molecules can have identical carbon skeletons and behave completely differently — because of a single functional group. One might be a fuel. The other might be a medication. The functional group is the difference.

There are over 20 named functional groups in organic chemistry. You don't need all of them today. As a future CRNA, six groups appear in almost every drug you'll ever administer. We're going to learn those six — deeply, visually, clinically.

03 · The Six Words

Your Core Vocabulary

Tap each group to expand it. Read through them in order — each one builds on the last.

Alcohol
— O — H  ·  the —OH group
water-lover
+
R — OH
  • ▸What it looks like: An oxygen atom bonded to a hydrogen, hanging off the carbon chain. Written as —OH. Always at a bend or endpoint.
  • ▸What it means: The OH group is hydrophilic — it loves water. Molecules with —OH dissolve in water readily. This determines how a drug distributes in blood and tissue.
  • ▸Recognize it instantly: Anywhere you see an O followed by H hanging off a carbon, that's an alcohol. The O and H are always written together: OH.
Clinical Example
Propofol (Diprivan)
The phenol ring in propofol contains an —OH group. This makes propofol slightly water-soluble despite being mostly lipid-soluble — a balance that helps it cross into the brain fast while still being injectable in an emulsion.
Amine
— N —  ·  nitrogen-containing
base · pH changer
+
R — NH₂  (primary)
R — NH — R  (secondary)
R — N — R₂  (tertiary)
  • ▸What it looks like: A nitrogen atom (N) bonded to the carbon chain, with 1, 2, or 3 hydrogens attached depending on type. Nitrogen has 3 bonds — count what's attached.
  • ▸What it means: Amines are basic — they raise pH. This is critical for anesthesia. Local anesthetics work by crossing cell membranes in their uncharged (base) form, then becoming charged inside the cell to block sodium channels.
  • ▸Recognize it instantly: See an N in a structure? That's almost always an amine. N is the giveaway.
Clinical Example
Lidocaine · Bupivacaine · Fentanyl
All local anesthetics and most opioids contain an amine group. The amine is literally the mechanism — it's what changes charge state to cross the nerve membrane. No amine, no block.
Carbonyl
C = O  ·  carbon double-bonded to oxygen
reaction hub
+
R — C = O — R  (ketone)
R — C = O — H  (aldehyde)
  • ▸What it looks like: A carbon with a double bond to oxygen. Written as C=O. The double bond is the key — one line means single bond, two lines means double bond. Oxygen pulling hard on those electrons.
  • ▸What it means: The C=O bond is polarized — the oxygen pulls electrons toward itself, making the carbon slightly positive. This makes it highly reactive — a site where other molecules attack. Many metabolic reactions happen here.
  • ▸Recognize it instantly: Two lines between C and O. That double bond is unmistakable once you know to look for it.
Clinical Example
Ketamine · Midazolam
Ketamine has a carbonyl group that contributes to its reactivity at NMDA receptors. Midazolam (the benzodiazepine you'll use for pre-op sedation) has a carbonyl that gets metabolized in the liver via that reactive carbon.
Carboxylic Acid
— COOH  ·  carbonyl + alcohol
acid · ionizer
+
R — C(=O) — OH

Or written compactly: R—COOH
  • ▸What it looks like: A carbonyl (C=O) with an OH right next to it on the same carbon. It's two groups fused together. COOH is always written together as a unit.
  • ▸What it means: It donates a hydrogen ion (H⁺) in solution, making it acidic. Many drugs are carboxylic acids — they ionize at body pH, which controls how they cross membranes and how fast they're eliminated by the kidneys.
  • ▸Recognize it instantly: Look for COOH as a unit, always at the end of a chain. It's the combination of C=O and OH on the same carbon that defines it.
Clinical Example
Aspirin (acetylsalicylic acid) · Ibuprofen
Both NSAIDs — drugs you'll manage peri-operatively — are carboxylic acids. Their COOH groups determine their half-life, renal clearance, and why they can cause stomach irritation (donating H⁺ in the stomach lining).
Ester
— COO —  ·  the hydrolysis target
metabolized fast
+
R — C(=O) — O — R
  • ▸What it looks like: Similar to carboxylic acid, but the OH is replaced by an O connecting to another carbon chain. Two carbons with an oxygen bridge between them, and a C=O on one side.
  • ▸What it means: Esters are cleaved by water (hydrolysis) — and by enzymes called esterases in the blood. This makes ester-containing drugs metabolize very quickly. Short duration, fast offset.
  • ▸Recognize it instantly: The —COO— pattern: carbonyl carbon, then an oxygen, then another carbon. That oxygen bridge between two carbons is the signature.
Clinical Example
Succinylcholine · Cocaine · Procaine
Succinylcholine — your rapid-sequence intubation drug — is an ester. Plasma cholinesterases cleave that ester bond almost immediately, which is exactly why it has such a short duration. Fast on, fast off. The ester is the mechanism of its brevity.
Aromatic Ring
benzene core  ·  the hexagon
stable · lipophilic
+
⬡   Six-carbon hexagon
with alternating double bonds
(often drawn as a circle inside a hexagon)
  • ▸What it looks like: A perfect hexagon, sometimes with a circle inside it, sometimes with alternating double bonds. Always 6 carbons. This is called a benzene ring.
  • ▸What it means: Aromatic rings are extremely stable — the electrons are delocalized around the whole ring rather than sitting in fixed bonds. This stability makes the molecule resistant to metabolism. It also makes it fat-soluble, helping it cross the blood-brain barrier.
  • ▸Recognize it instantly: A hexagon in any structure = benzene ring = aromatic. Once you know this shape, you'll see it in almost every psychoactive drug ever made.
Clinical Example
Propofol · Fentanyl · Midazolam · Lidocaine
Nearly every anesthetic agent in your future career contains an aromatic ring. The hexagon is what helps these drugs cross the blood-brain barrier efficiently. Propofol is essentially a benzene ring with two isopropyl groups and an —OH. That's the whole molecule.

06 · Clinical Connection

Reading a Drug Label Like a Chemist

Every drug name ending in -ol contains an alcohol. Every -amine or -caine contains an amine. Every -ate contains an ester. The name is a map. — Molecular Literacy, applied

Drug nomenclature is not arbitrary. The endings of drug names are often direct clues to their functional groups:

Name → Group → Behavior

-ol (propofol, metoprolol) → alcohol group → water interaction, liver metabolism

-aine / -caine (lidocaine, bupivcaine) → amine → membrane crossing, pH-dependent action

-ate (succinate, acetylate) → ester → rapid hydrolysis, short duration

-one (ketamine... wait, that's an amine) → ketone/carbonyl → reactive center, metabolic target

You now have enough vocabulary to start making educated guesses about how a drug will behave just from its name and rough structure — before you ever read the pharmacology chapter.

That's not a small thing. That's reading the molecule.
