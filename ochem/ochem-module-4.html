01 · The Final Layer

Languages Change. So Do Molecules.

You can read a molecule now. You know what its skeleton means, what its functional groups predict, how to tell a story about its behavior from structure alone. That is real literacy.

But molecules are not static. The body is not a library — it's a factory. The moment a drug enters the bloodstream, enzymes begin transforming it. Functional groups get modified. New groups get added. Bonds get broken. Sometimes the result is an inactive waste product. Sometimes it's a molecule more potent than what you started with.

Grammar is what happens when words interact. Metabolism is what happens when molecules meet the body. — Molecular Literacy, Module 4

Understanding molecular grammar — how reactions work and why — is what separates someone who knows drug names from someone who understands drug behavior. As a CRNA, you will be the person in the room who understands both.

02 · The Three Moves

How the Body Edits Molecules

The body has essentially three editing moves it applies to drugs. Learn these and you can predict what happens to almost any drug in the body.

What happened: The liver's CYP450 enzymes added an oxygen to a carbon. A methyl group (CH₃) became a hydroxymethyl group (CH₂OH) — an alcohol. This is the body's most common first edit on a drug.

Why it matters: Adding —OH does two things: it makes the molecule more water-soluble (easier to excrete in urine), and it creates a handle the liver can grab for Phase II processing. Oxidation is essentially the body saying: "I need to get rid of this — let me make it more water-soluble first."
Clinical Example
Midazolam is oxidized by CYP3A4 to 1-hydroxymidazolam — still pharmacologically active. This is why some patients get prolonged sedation: the metabolite is still working. If a patient is on a CYP3A4 inhibitor (like certain antifungals), midazolam isn't oxidized as fast — it accumulates. Understanding oxidation explains drug interactions before you memorize them.

What happened: Water (H₂O) was used to break the ester bond. The molecule was cut in two: a carboxylic acid and an alcohol. Both fragments are generally inactive and water-soluble — ready for excretion.

Why it matters: This is the fastest metabolic reaction in the body. Plasma esterases are everywhere in the blood. Any drug with an ester bond is a ticking clock. The ester is the duration timer — you saw this with succinylcholine in Module 3.
Clinical Example
Succinylcholine (2 ester bonds → cleaved in ~10 min), remifentanil (ester opioid → half-life of 3–4 minutes, shortest of any opioid), and cocaine (ester bonds → metabolized by plasma esterases). Ester = fast. Amide = slow. This single distinction explains why lidocaine lasts longer than procaine, why remifentanil disappears while fentanyl lingers.

What happened: A large, highly water-soluble molecule (glucuronic acid) was attached to the drug's —OH group. The drug is now enormous and polar — too big to cross membranes, too water-soluble to stay in tissues. It exits in urine or bile.

Why it matters: Conjugation is the body's final exit stamp. Phase I makes the drug grabbable. Phase II attaches the exit tag. Together they are the complete metabolic sentence: find it → modify it → tag it → flush it.
Clinical Example
Morphine undergoes glucuronidation to two products: morphine-3-glucuronide (inactive, may cause side effects) and morphine-6-glucuronide (MORE potent than morphine itself). In patients with renal failure, M6G accumulates because the kidneys can't excrete it — leading to prolonged, dangerously deep sedation. The grammar of metabolism becomes life-threatening pharmacology.
03 · The Liver's Workflow

Phase I → Phase II: The Full Sequence

Walk through the liver's complete processing sequence for a drug. Tap each step to reveal what's happening and why.

Drug Metabolism · Complete Pathway · Tap each step
Entry
Drug enters hepatic circulation
The drug arrives at the liver via the portal vein (oral drugs) or systemic circulation (IV drugs). Lipophilic drugs cross hepatocyte membranes easily — the more fat-soluble, the more liver exposure. This is the "first-pass effect" for oral medications: some drugs are so heavily metabolized by the liver on first pass that very little reaches systemic circulation. Propofol avoids this because it's given IV, bypassing the portal system entirely.
Phase I
CYP450 enzymes oxidize, reduce, or hydrolyze
Cytochrome P450 enzymes — embedded in the endoplasmic reticulum of liver cells — perform the first chemical edit. Usually oxidation: they add an —OH group or break a bond, making the molecule more polar. This often (not always) reduces activity. Some prodrugs are designed to be activated by this step — codeine to morphine is the classic example. The drug is inactive until CYP2D6 converts it.
Phase II
Conjugation attaches the exit tag
The Phase I metabolite — now bearing an —OH or other polar group — gets tagged with a large hydrophilic molecule: glucuronic acid (glucuronidation), sulfate (sulfation), or others. The resulting conjugate is large, water-soluble, and pharmacologically inactive. It cannot cross cell membranes. It is now cargo waiting for transport out of the body.
Exit
Renal or biliary excretion
Small conjugates exit in urine (renal). Large conjugates exit in bile (biliary) into the intestine. This is why kidney and liver function both matter for drug dosing — and why you will adjust doses for patients with renal or hepatic impairment. The exit route depends on molecular weight: below ~300–400 Da goes to kidney; above that tends toward bile. The grammar of the molecule determines how it leaves.
Exception
Active metabolites — the plot twist
Not all metabolites are inactive. Morphine-6-glucuronide is more potent than morphine. Normeperidine (meperidine metabolite) causes seizures. Fentanyl's metabolite norfentanyl is inactive — which is why fentanyl has a cleaner, more predictable offset. When you understand the metabolic grammar of each drug, these aren't surprises — they're logical conclusions written in the structure from the start.
