⚠ CRITICAL VALUES REQUIRE IMMEDIATE MD NOTIFICATION — DO NOT DELAY
WBC — White Blood CellsLeukocytes
4.5–11.0 K/µLCRIT: <2 or >30
▲ HIGH — LeukocytosisInfection, inflammation, leukemia, steroid use, stress response
▼ LOW — LeukopeniaBone marrow suppression, chemo, HIV, autoimmune disease
⬡ NURSING PRIORITYCheck differential — focus on Neutrophils (ANC). If ANC <500: Neutropenic Precautions (private room, no fresh flowers/fruit, mask). Monitor for fever ≥38°C (100.4°F) as first sign of infection.
Hemoglobin (Hgb)Oxygen-carrying protein in RBCs
M: 13.5–17.5 g/dL
F: 12.0–15.5 g/dLCRIT: <7.0 g/dL
▲ HIGH — PolycythemiaDehydration, COPD, high altitude adaptation, polycythemia vera
▼ LOW — AnemiaBlood loss, iron deficiency, B12/folate deficiency, hemolysis, CKD
⬡ NURSING PRIORITYHgb <7: prepare transfusion protocol, monitor O2 sat, HR, BP. Hgb <8 with symptoms: O2 therapy, activity restrictions. Assess for dyspnea, fatigue, pallor, tachycardia.
Hematocrit (Hct)% of blood volume that is RBCs
M: 41–53%
F: 36–46%
Roughly 3× the Hgb value. Drops in anemia/hemorrhage, rises in dehydration. Trending Hct is key in GI bleeds.
⬡ NURSING PRIORITYTrending matters more than a single value. Rapid drop = active bleeding. Monitor serial CBC every 4–6 hrs in acute hemorrhage.
Platelets (PLT)Thrombocytes — clotting
150–450 K/µLCRIT: <50 or >1000
▲ HIGH — ThrombocytosisInfection, iron deficiency, post-splenectomy, inflammatory disease. Risk of clot.
▼ LOW — ThrombocytopeniaHeparin-induced (HIT), ITP, chemo, liver disease, sepsis. Spontaneous bleeding risk.
⬡ NURSING PRIORITYPLT <50K: BLEEDING PRECAUTIONS — electric razor, soft toothbrush, no IM injections, fall precautions. PLT <20K: spontaneous intracranial bleed risk — prepare platelet transfusion. Monitor petechiae, ecchymosis, guaiac stool.
RBC CountRed Blood Cell count
M: 4.5–5.9 M/µL
F: 4.0–5.2 M/µL
Reflects total number of RBCs. Used with Hgb/Hct to classify type of anemia (microcytic, macrocytic, normocytic).
⬡ NURSING PRIORITYUse MCV alongside RBC: MCV <80 = microcytic (iron deficiency). MCV >100 = macrocytic (B12/folate). Normal MCV with low RBC = normocytic (blood loss, CKD).
⚠ ELECTROLYTE CRITICAL VALUES → CARDIAC MONITORING REQUIRED
// ELECTROLYTES
Sodium (Na+) ⚡ SUDDEN DEATHPrimary extracellular electrolyte
135–145 mEq/LCRIT: <120 or >160
▲ HIGH — HypernatremiaDehydration, diabetes insipidus, excessive sodium intake. Causes cellular shrinkage.
▼ LOW — HyponatremiaExcess water intake, SIADH, heart failure, liver cirrhosis. Causes cellular swelling → SEIZURE RISK.
⬡ NURSING PRIORITYCorrect SLOWLY — rapid correction causes osmotic demyelination (brain damage). Seizure precautions if <125. Assess neuro status: confusion, lethargy, seizures. Restrict free water in SIADH.
Potassium (K+) ⚡ SUDDEN DEATHThe Cardiac Electrolyte
3.5–5.0 mEq/LCRIT: <2.5 or >6.5
▲ HIGH — HyperkalemiaRenal failure, ACE inhibitors, K-sparing diuretics, tissue destruction, acidosis
▼ LOW — HypokalemiaDiuretics (furosemide), vomiting, diarrhea, alkalosis, poor intake
⬡ NURSING PRIORITYBOTH high AND low cause life-threatening arrhythmias. GET EKG IMMEDIATELY. NEVER IV push K+ — always dilute, max 10 mEq/hr peripheral. Low K+: replace with oral or IV. High K+: Kayexalate, insulin+dextrose, calcium gluconate (cardiac protection), dialysis.
Calcium (Ca2+)Bone, muscle, nerve function
8.5–10.5 mg/dLCRIT: <7.0 or >13.0
▲ HIGH — HypercalcemiaHyperparathyroidism, bone metastases, prolonged immobility. "Bones, groans, stones, moans."
▼ LOW — HypocalcemiaHypoparathyroidism, vitamin D deficiency, pancreatitis, post-thyroidectomy
⬡ NURSING PRIORITYLow Ca2+: Chvostek's sign (tap facial nerve → twitch) and Trousseau's sign (BP cuff → carpal spasm) = positive = TETANY RISK. Seizure precautions. Have IV calcium gluconate at bedside post-thyroidectomy.
Magnesium (Mg2+)Muscle/nerve function, K+ regulation
1.5–2.5 mEq/LCRIT: <1.0 or >5.0
▲ HIGH — HypermagnesemiaRenal failure, excess Mg antacids/laxatives. Causes CNS depression, respiratory failure.
▼ LOW — HypomagnesemiaAlcoholism, malnutrition, diuretics, diarrhea. Often co-exists with low K+ and Ca2+.
⬡ NURSING PRIORITYMg is the "forgotten electrolyte." Always check when K+ or Ca2+ are abnormal — hard to correct K+ without fixing Mg first. High Mg toxicity antidote: CALCIUM GLUCONATE IV. Monitor deep tendon reflexes (DTRs) — loss of DTRs = early toxicity sign.
// GLUCOSE & RENAL
Blood Glucose (BG)Blood sugar level
Fasting: 70–99 mg/dL
Random: <140 mg/dLCRIT: <40 or >500
▲ HIGH — HyperglycemiaDiabetes, steroids, stress response, TPN, pancreatitis. DKA if >250 with ketones.
▼ LOW — HypoglycemiaToo much insulin, missed meals, alcohol, sepsis. Brain uses ONLY glucose — damage within minutes.
⬡ NURSING PRIORITYHypoglycemia is the PRIORITY — treat first, ask questions later. Conscious: 15g fast carbs (OJ, glucose tabs). Unconscious: D50 IV or glucagon IM. Recheck in 15 min. Never leave patient alone during hypoglycemic event.
BUN — Blood Urea NitrogenKidney waste product
7–20 mg/dL
Measures kidney waste clearance. Also elevated in dehydration, GI bleed, and high protein diet. Less specific than creatinine alone.
⬡ NURSING PRIORITYBUN:Creatinine ratio >20:1 = pre-renal cause (dehydration, reduced perfusion). Ratio <10:1 = intrinsic renal disease. Fluid resuscitation often fixes pre-renal azotemia. Monitor I&O strictly.
Creatinine (Cr)Gold standard kidney marker
M: 0.7–1.3 mg/dL
F: 0.5–1.1 mg/dLCRIT: >10 mg/dL
▲ HIGHRenal failure (AKI or CKD), rhabdomyolysis, dehydration, nephrotoxic drugs
▼ LOWLow muscle mass, malnutrition, advanced liver disease (rarely clinically significant)
⬡ NURSING PRIORITYCreatinine >2× baseline = AKI. HOLD nephrotoxic meds: NSAIDs, aminoglycosides, IV contrast, ACE inhibitors. Monitor strict I&O — urine output <0.5 mL/kg/hr = oliguria. Notify MD. Prepare for possible dialysis.
⚠ ELEVATED CARDIAC MARKERS + CHEST PAIN = POTENTIAL MI — ACTIVATE PROTOCOL IMMEDIATELY
Troponin I / Troponin T ⚡ SUDDEN DEATHGOLD STANDARD for MI detection
Normal: <0.04 ng/mLANY elevation = CRITICAL
Most specific marker for myocardial damage. Rises 3–6 hrs after injury, peaks at 24 hrs, stays elevated 7–14 days. Serial troponins every 3–6 hrs are key.
⬡ NURSING PRIORITYANY positive troponin = cardiac event until proven otherwise. 12-lead EKG STAT. Continuous cardiac monitoring. IV access × 2. O2 if SpO2 <94%. Aspirin 325mg (unless contraindicated). Prepare for cath lab activation. NPO status. Do NOT leave patient unattended.
CK-MBCreatine Kinase — Myocardial Band
Normal: <5% total CK
<25 U/L
Rises 4–8 hrs post-MI, peaks at 18–24 hrs, normalizes in 72 hrs. Useful for detecting reinfarction because it normalizes faster than troponin.
⬡ NURSING PRIORITYUsed alongside troponin. If troponin elevated but CK-MB normal — consider older MI. If CK-MB re-elevates after normalizing — suspect reinfarction. Monitor chest pain pattern, radiation, diaphoresis.
BNP / NT-proBNPBrain Natriuretic Peptide — Heart Failure Marker
BNP: <100 pg/mL
NT-proBNP: <300 pg/mL
▲ HIGHHeart failure (ventricular stretch), fluid overload, pulmonary embolism, renal failure
▼ LOW / NORMALEffectively rules OUT heart failure as cause of dyspnea (high negative predictive value)
⬡ NURSING PRIORITYBNP >400 = high probability HF. Trending BNP guides treatment response — decreasing = responding to diuresis. Daily weights, strict I&O, fluid restriction per order, assess for edema/crackles/JVD.
Lipid PanelCholesterol profile
Total Chol: <200 mg/dL
LDL: <100 mg/dL
HDL: >60 mg/dL
TG: <150 mg/dL
LDL = "Lousy" cholesterol — deposits in arteries. HDL = "Healthy" — removes cholesterol. High triglycerides = pancreatitis risk if >500.
⬡ NURSING PRIORITYMust fast 9–12 hrs before draw (especially for accurate TG). Statin therapy monitoring: check LFTs — myopathy risk (muscle pain + elevated CK = rhabdo risk). Counsel on diet, exercise, smoking cessation.
⚠ CRITICAL COAG VALUES = SEVERE BLEEDING OR CLOTTING RISK — NOTIFY MD STAT
PT — Prothrombin TimeExtrinsic pathway / Warfarin monitoring
11–13.5 secondsCRIT: >35 sec
Measures how long blood takes to clot via the extrinsic pathway. Prolonged by warfarin, liver disease, Vitamin K deficiency, DIC.
⬡ NURSING PRIORITYUsed to monitor WARFARIN therapy. Target PT usually 1.5–2.5× normal. Antidote: Vitamin K (slow) or FFP (fast). Assess for bleeding: gums, urine, stool, bruising. Hold warfarin if supratherapeutic — call MD.
INR — International Normalized RatioStandardized PT ratio
Normal: 0.8–1.2
Therapeutic (anticoag): 2.0–3.0CRIT: >5.0
Standardized version of PT used globally. INR 2–3 = therapeutic anticoagulation for A-fib, DVT, PE. INR 2.5–3.5 = mechanical heart valves.
⬡ NURSING PRIORITYINR >5 = HIGH bleed risk. Hold warfarin, notify MD. INR >9 = consider Vitamin K reversal. Check ALL medications — many drugs affect INR (antibiotics, NSAIDs, OTC meds). Instruct patients never to start/stop meds without telling provider.
aPTT — Activated Partial Thromboplastin TimeIntrinsic pathway / Heparin monitoring
Normal: 25–35 sec
Therapeutic: 60–100 secCRIT: >120 sec
Measures intrinsic clotting pathway. Used to monitor UNFRACTIONATED HEPARIN drip therapy. Prolonged in hemophilia, DIC, heparin excess.
⬡ NURSING PRIORITYCheck aPTT every 6 hrs when on heparin drip. Adjust per protocol/nomogram. ANTIDOTE: Protamine sulfate. Watch for HIT (heparin-induced thrombocytopenia) — paradoxical CLOTTING with low platelets. If platelets drop >50% → STOP heparin immediately.
D-DimerFibrin degradation product
Normal: <500 ng/mL
(0.5 µg/mL FEU)
Elevated when clots are forming and breaking down — sensitive but NOT specific. Good for RULING OUT DVT/PE. Elevated in pregnancy, surgery, infection, cancer, trauma.
⬡ NURSING PRIORITYNormal D-dimer effectively rules out DVT/PE in low-risk patients. Elevated D-dimer requires imaging (CT-PA for PE, duplex ultrasound for DVT). Assess for calf pain, swelling (DVT) or sudden dyspnea, pleuritic chest pain (PE).
⚠ SEVERE LFT ELEVATION = POSSIBLE ACUTE LIVER FAILURE — MONITOR FOR ENCEPHALOPATHY
ALT — Alanine AminotransferaseLiver-specific enzyme
7–56 U/L
Most specific marker for hepatocellular (liver cell) damage. Elevated in hepatitis, acetaminophen toxicity, alcohol liver disease, fatty liver, ischemia.
⬡ NURSING PRIORITYALT >3× normal = significant liver injury. >10× = severe. Assess for jaundice, RUQ pain, dark urine, clay-colored stools. HOLD hepatotoxic drugs (acetaminophen limit 2g/day, statins). Alcohol counseling. Monitor coagulation — liver makes clotting factors.
AST — Aspartate AminotransferaseLiver + cardiac enzyme
10–40 U/L
Less specific than ALT — also elevated in cardiac, muscle, and kidney damage. AST:ALT ratio >2:1 suggests alcoholic liver disease.
⬡ NURSING PRIORITYAlways interpret with ALT. AST/ALT ratio helps differentiate alcoholic hepatitis (ratio >2) from viral hepatitis (ALT > AST). If cardiac cause suspected, correlate with troponin and CK.
Bilirubin (Total)Breakdown product of heme
Total: 0.1–1.2 mg/dL
Direct: 0–0.3 mg/dL
Indirect: 0.1–1.0 mg/dL
▲ HIGH — Jaundice >2.5Pre-hepatic (hemolysis), hepatic (hepatitis, cirrhosis), post-hepatic (bile duct obstruction, gallstones)
▼ LOWClinically insignificant
⬡ NURSING PRIORITYAssess skin and sclerae (whites of eyes) for jaundice — scleral icterus appears first. In neonates: phototherapy initiated per protocol (jaundice can cause kernicterus/brain damage). Direct bili elevated = obstruction → assess for dark urine, clay stools.
AlbuminProtein made by liver — nutritional/hepatic marker
3.5–5.0 g/dLCRIT: <2.0 g/dL
Reflects liver synthetic function and nutritional status. Low albumin = edema (decreased oncotic pressure), poor wound healing, altered drug binding (toxic levels possible).
⬡ NURSING PRIORITYLow albumin alters drug pharmacokinetics — many drugs (phenytoin, warfarin) are albumin-bound. Check for ascites, peripheral edema, poor wound healing. Nutritional consult for albumin <3. Pressure ulcer prevention critical in hypoalbuminemia.
Ammonia (NH3)Byproduct of protein metabolism
15–45 mcg/dLCRIT: >200 mcg/dL
Liver converts ammonia to urea. In liver failure, ammonia accumulates → hepatic encephalopathy (confusion, asterixis, coma).
⬡ NURSING PRIORITYElevated ammonia = neuro assessment priority. Check for asterixis (flapping tremor — ask patient to extend arms/wrists). Safety: fall precautions, call light within reach. Lactulose is treatment of choice — goal: 2–3 soft stools/day to trap and excrete ammonia.

▲ HIGH (>1.030)Dehydration, SIADH, DM, contrast media — urine is concentrated
▼ LOW (<1.005)Overhydration, diabetes insipidus, renal tubular damage — urine is dilute

⬡ NURSING PRIORITYQuick bedside assessment of hydration. Encourage fluids if high. In DI: monitor for massive urine output (may be 10–20L/day). Compare with serum osmolality for complete picture.
Urine pHAcid-base of urine
4.5–8.0
(Normal ~6.0)
Acidic urine (<6): high protein diet, acidosis, UTI with certain bacteria, cranberry juice. Alkaline urine (>7): UTI (Proteus), vegetarian diet, metabolic alkalosis, kidney tubular acidosis.
⬡ NURSING PRIORITYAlkaline urine promotes calcium stone formation. Acidic urine promotes uric acid stones. In kidney stone patients: target urine pH per MD order. Hydration is KEY — goal urine output 2–3L/day for stone prevention.
Urine ProteinShould NOT be present normally
Normal: Negative / trace
<150 mg/24hr
Proteinuria signals glomerular damage (protein leaks through damaged filters). Present in nephrotic syndrome, diabetic nephropathy, hypertension, preeclampsia.
⬡ NURSING PRIORITYIn pregnancy: >300mg/24hr = preeclampsia → monitor BP, reflexes, CNS symptoms, fetal heart tones. Dipstick 3+ or 4+ = significant. Collect 24-hr urine accurately — save ALL urine on ice. Nephrotic syndrome: assess for massive edema, low albumin.
Urine WBCs / Leukocyte EsteraseInfection marker
Normal: <5 WBC/hpf
LE: Negative
Elevated WBCs in urine = pyuria = UTI or kidney infection until proven otherwise. LE positive is rapid screening test — confirm with culture and sensitivity (C&S).
⬡ NURSING PRIORITYALWAYS get urine C&S BEFORE starting antibiotics. Mid-stream clean catch technique is critical for accurate results. Catheter-associated UTI (CAUTI) prevention: maintain closed system, peri-care, remove catheter ASAP. In elderly: UTI often presents as CONFUSION, not classic symptoms.
// RENAL FUNCTION
eGFR — Estimated Glomerular Filtration RateBest overall kidney function indicator
Normal: >90 mL/min/1.73m²CRIT: <15 = Kidney Failure
Calculated from creatinine, age, and sex (CKD-EPI 2021 formula — race-neutral as of 2021/2022 update). Stages CKD: Stage 1 (>90, normal), Stage 2 (60–89), Stage 3 (30–59, moderate), Stage 4 (15–29, severe), Stage 5 (<15, kidney failure).
⬡ NURSING PRIORITYeGFR <30: dose-adjust or HOLD renally-cleared drugs (metformin, many antibiotics, opioids, NSAIDs). eGFR <15: dialysis planning begins. Prepare for AV fistula placement (protect that arm — NO BP, NO blood draws). Dietary restrictions: low K+, low phosphorus, fluid restriction.
Urine Output (UO)Hourly renal perfusion monitor
Normal: 0.5–1 mL/kg/hr
Min acceptable: 30 mL/hr
▲ HIGH — PolyuriaDiabetes insipidus, hyperglycemia, diuretics, recovering AKI
▼ LOW — Oliguria <400 mL/dayDehydration, AKI, cardiogenic shock, sepsis, obstruction
⬡ NURSING PRIORITYUO <30 mL/hr for 2 consecutive hours = NOTIFY MD. First assess: is catheter kinked? Is patient dehydrated? Give fluid bolus per order. No response to fluids = suspect AKI or cardiac cause. Anuria (<50 mL/day) = emergency — dialysis may be needed urgently.
